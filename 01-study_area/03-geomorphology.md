# Geomorphology and Soil Types

> *Extracted from: Evaluation of Water Quality Index for River Mogral (2021)*
> *Source pages (PDF): 13, 14*

The Kasaragod district can be divided into three major physiographic regions: the coastal plains, the midlands, and the eastern highland regions.

The coastal plains consist of narrow belts of alluvial deposits with elevations generally below 10 m above mean sea level and occur parallel to the coastline. To the east of the coastal belt lies the midland region, where elevations range between 10 and 300 m above mean sea level. This region is characterized by rugged terrain composed of small hillocks separated by deep valleys and generally slopes toward the western coast.

Further east lies the highland region, which forms part of the elevated terrain associated with the Western Ghats. The midland and hill regions present a rolling landscape with hills largely composed of lateritic formations, while valley areas are covered by valley fill deposits consisting mainly of colluvium and alluvium.

Four major soil types occur within the district:

* **Lateritic soil**, the most dominant soil type, occurring in the midland and hilly regions and derived from lateritic formations.
* **Brown hydromorphic soil**, found in valleys and low-lying areas of the midland region and coastal strip, formed through transportation and sedimentation of materials from surrounding hill slopes.
* **Alluvial soil**, present in the western coastal tract of the district.
* **Forest loam**, occurring in the eastern hilly regions and characterized by a surface layer rich in organic matter.

The coastal plain is often characterized by sandy secondary soils with relatively poor water-holding capacity.

The hydrogeology of the district consists of four major hydrogeological units:

* Coastal alluvium
* Laterites
* Weathered crystalline formations
* Fractured crystalline formations

Coastal alluvium occurs as narrow strips parallel to the coast south of Kasaragod, with the width of these deposits increasing toward the south and reaching approximately 5 km around Trikaripur. North of Kasaragod, particularly in the Kasaragod and Manjeshwar blocks, the alluvium occurs as isolated patches near the coast and generally has limited thickness. In the Kanhangad and Nileshwar blocks, although the width of alluvium is greater, groundwater potential zones are mainly found in the upper portions of the deposits.
